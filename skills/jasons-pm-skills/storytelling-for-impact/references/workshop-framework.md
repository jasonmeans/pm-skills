# Storytelling for Impact — Full Workshop Framework

This reference document contains the complete workshop framework from Jason's "Storytelling for Impact" workshop, including all exercises, evaluation criteria, and delivery coaching.

## Why Story?

Stories serve a specific function in business contexts:

- **Lead Change** - Stories create clarity and generate energy to drive transformation
- **Deliver Success** - Stories help solve complex problems and address user dissatisfaction
- **Have Impact** - Stories produce outcomes by winning hearts and minds

### What a story IS:
- A way to connect with other humans
- A tool to transmit knowledge
- A means to win hearts and minds
- A vehicle to confer meaning, establish context, create clarity, and generate energy

### What a story IS NOT:
- A product or service description
- A scenario with a persona
- A value proposition or OKR

### When to tell a story:
- Open with a story (hook the audience)
- Illustrate a point (make abstract concepts concrete)
- Close with a story (leave a lasting impression)

## The Neuroscience of Storytelling

### Brain Activation
- **Facts and data** activate only two brain regions (Broca's area and Wernicke's area — language processing)
- **Stories** activate multiple brain regions including motor cortex, sensory cortex, frontal cortex, and more
- Words like "perfume" or "coffee" activate the **primary olfactory cortex** — the brain simulates the experience

### Hormonal Response
- **Cortisol** is released during the rising arc of the story, prompting a powerful emotional reaction even when the listener knows the story is fiction
- **Oxytocin** floods the brain during character-driven stories, producing feelings of love and attachment

### Key Insight
- The brain **ignores** cliched or overused words and phrases — scientists theorize this is caused by a lack of storytelling power
- The "Hero's Journey" story model is the foundation for half of all Hollywood movies and the majority of the most-watched TED talks
- Only 5% of people remember statistics after a presentation, but 63% remember stories (Sue Desmond-Hellmann, CEO of Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation)

### The "Recall a Childhood Smell" Exercise
Ask participants to recall a childhood smell. This demonstrates how sensory language activates the brain's simulation capabilities — the same mechanism that makes stories more memorable than facts.

> "Opposable thumbs let us hang on; story told us what to hang on to." — Lisa Cron

## The Curse of Knowledge

The storyteller faces a fundamental tension:

- **You** are the actor performing for the audience
- **The audience** is who matters — the story is for them, not you
- **The Curse of Knowledge** means you know too much — you must resist the urge to include everything you know
- **Hold the line** — stay focused on what the audience needs

## Story Shape Models

### Kurt Vonnegut's Story Shapes
Vonnegut identified recurring shapes that stories follow:

1. **Rags to Riches** - Starting low, rising steadily
2. **Tragedy** - Starting high, falling steadily
3. **Man in a Hole** - Starting okay, falling into trouble, climbing back out
4. **Icarus** - Rising high, then crashing down
5. **Cinderella** - Down, up, down, up (the most versatile for business)
6. **Oedipus** - Complex trajectory with revelation and reversal

Source: "The Shapes of Stories," Andy Reagan, Computational Story Lab, University of Vermont

### The Cinderella Shape for Pitch Perfect
The Cinderella story shape is especially effective for business pitches:

1. **Down**: Outline the problem — what is broken or at risk
2. **Up**: Present the solution to address the problem
3. **Down**: Acknowledge the cost — what it will take
4. **Up**: Promise the future if we deliver

### The Analogous Opener
A technique for synthesizing complex problems into simple mental models:

- Open with a relatable analogy from everyday life
- Consider introducing personal experiences to make it authentic
- It is not always about you — telling stories about other people is fine
- **Risk**: You can lose the audience if you linger — jump to business quickly
- Cars and food are particularly effective analogy sources

**Example from the workshop**: "Driving from Everett to Alaska then to the Florida Keys would take 22 days. It takes about the same amount of time to get your team's code to merge!" — This analogy makes an abstract engineering metric viscerally understandable.

### The Hero's Journey
Use the Hero's Journey model when:
- You need a human element in the story
- You are influencing different behaviors
- You are dealing with a big problem or "abyss"

## The Story Map — Complete Exercise Details

### Step 1: Define the Impact

**The Prompt**: "We need to tell a story about ______ so that we can ______."

**Common impact categories**:
- Influence others to work constructively with you
- Gain buy-in from key stakeholders
- Draw attention to value in your work that others may not see
- Get needed resources from management
- Help people understand what drives and motivates the people on this team

**Diagnostic questions**:
- Why do you need to tell a story?
- What business problem(s) exist that this story can help to address?
- What specific outcomes do you want to drive?
- What data or critical information should be included, if any?
- How will you know that the story is landing?

**Key rule**: If you cannot define the impact and your audience, do not waste your time and energy on creating a story.

### Step 2: Know Your Audience

**Define who your audience is** (examples):
- Leadership Team
- Software Engineers
- Users / Customers
- Partner Team
- Data Scientists
- Designers
- User Research

**Tip**: The more you target, the harder it will be for the story to land. Targeting the highest-leveled person in the room or person with the least knowledge can help.

**Describe the typical person** in your audience:
- What job titles or roles do they have?
- What do they value most from this team?
- Does your work help them be more successful?

**Define psychographics**:
- Describe "what kind of people they are"
- What do they deeply value?
- What does "success" mean to them?
- What brings them joy?
- What brings them pain?
- What do they dread?
- What is their "pet peeve"?
- What annoys the heck out of them?
- What is meaningful to them?
- What do they aspire to accomplish?
- What are their most admirable qualities?
- What are their most negative qualities?

**Imagine how your audience views you**:
- What do you believe they think of you (and this team) and why?
- What biases or misconceptions exist, good or bad?
- Are their perceptions and opinions valid?

**Define where you want to take your audience**:
- What do you want them to understand?
- What emotions do you want them to feel?
- How much do you care that they like you?
- What do you want them to do?
- What barriers exist to prevent the desired outcome?

**Think about the substance of the story**:
- How much does your audience care about it and why?
- In what ways does it represent an opportunity for your audience?
- In what ways does it represent a threat or danger?
- What is potentially the most surprising aspect?

### Step 3: Explore the Landscape

**Instructions**: Review the questions below. Note which ones elicit an immediate emotional response. Flag three to six questions and explain details.

**Think of the audience** (cite specific anecdotes or incidents):
- Have you ever had a frustrating or infuriating experience working with them?
- What pain in their lives goes away because of what this team does?
- What are good things now possible for them?
- What superpowers do you unlock in them?
- Have you ever seen them involved in a disaster?
- Do they ever miss stuff they need to know?
- Is there one person you admire?
- What is the worst experience you've had in working with them?
- What assumptions do you have about the audience?

**Think of the culture and experiences of your team**:
- What achievement (as an individual or team) are you most proud of?
- Have you ever said, "This is why our work is important"?
- Describe your best professional win
- What failure or disaster did you learn the most from?
- What is the hardest thing this team has ever had to do?
- What is the best thing this team has done to help another human being?
- What is the team's proudest moment?
- What has been the greatest surprise resulting from this team's work?
- What was your best team adventure?
- What is your team's guilty pleasure?

### Step 4: Discover the Gold

From the landscape exploration, you should have a list of potential story "nuggets." Evaluate each using:

**Interest — Is it compelling?**
A compelling story is rich with drama. Score against these five criteria (must be high on at least three):

| Criterion | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|
| Conflict/Stress | There is a problem. Something goes wrong. Something important is at risk. |
| Emotion | Any number of heightened emotions generated, and they cannot all be positive. |
| Resolution | The conflict builds to a climax, and things change in fundamental ways. |
| Surprise | Something unexpected happens — could be in the conflict, the resolution, or elsewhere. |
| Learning | The experience leaves you with new knowledge, insights, or awareness. |

**Audience — What will they like?**
Review demographic and psychographic details from Step 2 against each nugget.

**Relevance — Does it map to your story objective?**
Refer back to the impact statement from Step 1.

**Authenticity — Is it you?**
Evaluate both objectively (is the lesson relevant? is the story integral to the group?) and subjectively (do the emotions resonate with deeply held values and beliefs?). If the evaluation does not energize you with importance, it is probably not worth pursuing.

### Step 5: Shape the Story

Define the dramatic arc elements:

**The Dramatic Arc**:
1. **Exposition** - Setting the scene
2. **Rising Action** - Building tension
3. **Climax** - The turning point
4. **Falling Action** - Consequences unfold
5. **Resolution** - New normal, lesson emerges

**Specific elements to define**:

**Place and Time**:
- Where does it take place?
- Date and timeframe
- Location(s)
- Settings (house, building, office)
- Defining features
- Your relation to this place and point in time

**Problem (the inciting incident)**:
- What goes wrong, or is unexpectedly challenging?
- Who or what is responsible?
- Describe the moment in vivid terms
- What emotions does this generate?

**Complications**:
- Two or three factors that add drama
- Problems or circumstances that escalate the tension

**Climax and Resolution**:
- The moment conflict reaches its peak
- The turning point — everything before leads to it, everything after responds to it

### Step 6: Bring it to Life

**Write a headline**:
- Informative, provocative, and true to the essential point
- Contains a verb
- Does not include data
- No more than ten words

**Write the opening paragraph**:
- Encapsulates the challenge and scope
- Calls out at least one major insight
- Related to a business challenge, presented in human terms
- No longer than 75 words

**Identify your "pearls"**:
- List key events that happen in the story
- String them together to achieve the objective

**Sequence the dramatic elements**:
1. **Open**: Set the stage
2. **Key Moment**: Take the audience to a point-in-time experience
3. **Problem**: Explain what's wrong
4. **Resolution**: Unfold the drama
5. **Close**: Reflect on the lesson or "moral of the story"

## Delivery Coaching

### Four Dimensions of Delivery
- **Verbal**: Action words and physical language
- **Vocal**: Tempo changes, inflections, pauses, volume, attitudes
- **Physical**: Gestures, movement, eye contact, non-verbal reactions
- **Emotional**: Feelings and emotions that underlie the story

### Self-Evaluation Questions
As you practice and listen to others:
- Is the conflict/obstacle dramatic and clear enough?
- What does the main character want/desire?
- What does the telling of the story say about the person telling it?
- What does the story make you think about / reflect on internally?
- What details can be left out because they are unnecessary?

### Presentation Considerations
- **Location/Audience**: Hallway, Zoom, conference room; Engineer, Director, VP
- **Constraints**: Team meeting, 1:1, All Hands
- **Props and Mediums**: Slides, spoken story, video, written story
- **Preparation**: Rehearse. Rehearse. Rehearse.

### Visual Design Tips (for slide-supported stories)
- Think like a designer — typography, size, color, layout, and style matter
- Use the Fibonacci sequence / golden ratio for visual composition
- Be consistent with layout across slides
- Use high-quality, large, transparent images
- License images if needed
- Learn which graph types work for different data
- Keep videos short; use a script
- Cut anything that does not contribute to the story — use the appendix for detail slides

### Sharing and Feedback
- Get feedback and listen to it
- Share with confidence and stay authentic
- Stories are portable and can be retold by listeners — great stories become viral
- Stories provide context which helps create clarity, even in ambiguous spaces
- Stories generate energy and build excitement and support
