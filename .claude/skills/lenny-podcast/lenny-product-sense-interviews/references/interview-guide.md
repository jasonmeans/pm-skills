## Interview Structure and Time Management

- **Meta interviews**: 45 minutes total. **Google**: ~50 minutes
- 5 min intro, 35-40 min core framework, 5 min for your questions
- Speaking time should be 80-90% of total
- Keep a visible timer

### Time Allocation

| Section | Time |
|---------|------|
| Assumptions + Game Plan | 1-2 min |
| Product Motivation & Mission | <5 min |
| Ecosystem Players + Segmentation | <10 min |
| Problem Identification | <10 min |
| Solution Development | <10 min |
| V1 + Risks | ~1.5 min |

## Evaluation Framework (Detailed)

### Dimension 1: Product Motivation & Mission (Weight: 20%)

**5/5 Requirements:**
- Clear product fundamentals with strong company mission connection
- Compelling market gaps identified
- Deep insight into "why now"
- Specific, actionable mission statement

**Common Issues:**
- Generic missions that could apply to any product
- Missing status quo analysis
- Weak company strategy connection
- Insufficient market context

### Dimension 2: Target Audience (Weight: 25%)

**5/5 Requirements:**
- Comprehensive ecosystem players identified
- Clear rationale for selected group
- Well-defined mutually exclusive segments based on motivations/behaviors
- Strong prioritization framework (Reach + Underserved Degree)
- Vivid, specific persona

**Common Issues:**
- Overlapping segments
- Missing key stakeholders
- Weak prioritization rationale
- Overly broad or demographic-only segments

### Dimension 3: Problem Identification (Weight: 25%)

**5/5 Requirements:**
- Complete user journey mapping
- Distinct problems tied to journey steps
- Clear severity/frequency scoring
- Problems align with target segment
- Strong prioritization rationale connected to mission

**Common Issues:**
- Listing needs instead of problems
- Similar/overlapping problems
- Missing journey steps
- Weak prioritization

### Dimension 4: Solution Development (Weight: 20%)

**5/5 Requirements:**
- Three distinct approaches (different angles, not variations)
- Clear impact/effort evaluation
- Solutions directly address the prioritized problem
- Detailed MVP description
- Thoughtful risk analysis with mitigations

**Common Issues:**
- Solutions don't solve the core problem
- Similar solutions with minor variations
- Missing feasibility assessment
- Weak MVP description

### Dimension 5: Communication Structure (Weight: 10%)

**5/5 Requirements:**
- Clear section transitions with "waypointing"
- Regular check-ins with interviewer
- Efficient time management
- Structured thinking visible throughout
- Clear prioritization frameworks at each decision point

**Common Issues:**
- Poor time management (running out of time for solutions)
- Missing check-ins
- Unclear structure or rambling
- Not driving the interview

## Segmentation Heuristics Reference

Strong segmentation goes beyond demographics. Use these to create meaningful segments:

- **Primary motivations**: What fundamental goals drive their behavior?
- **Behavioral patterns**: How frequently and in what ways do they interact?
- **Context of use**: Where, when, and how do they engage?
- **Expertise level**: Novices, intermediate users, or experts?
- **Resource constraints**: Time, money, knowledge, space limitations?
- **Goals and outcomes**: What specific results are they trying to achieve?

### Product Archetypes for Preparation

Practice with these common product types:
- Two-sided marketplaces (Uber, Airbnb)
- Social feeds (Instagram, TikTok, Facebook)
- Messaging apps (WhatsApp, Messenger)
- Fintech products (payments, banking)
- Content platforms (Netflix, YouTube)

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

- Confusing needs with problems
- Shallow or demographic-only segmentation
- Thinking too tactically vs. strategically
- Jumping to solutions before fully defining the problem
- Contradicting yourself or your mission statement
- Not tying solution back to identified problem
- Introducing metrics discussions (save for AT interviews)
- Failing to state assumptions upfront
- Skipping ecosystem player analysis
- Creating segments that aren't meaningfully different
- Prematurely narrowing solution space
- Losing track of time
- Making it a "first-person problem" rather than using personas
- Asking "where would you like me to go next?" instead of driving
- Defensive responses to nudges
- Spending too much time on rejected options
- Using generic user journeys

## Google vs. Meta Differences

- Google blends evaluation dimensions ("hats"); Meta keeps them separate
- Google types: Product Insight, Strategic Insights, Craft and Execution, Analytical Thinking, Cross-functional Collaboration, Googliness and Leadership
- Google Product Insight: more user-focused, less monetization
- Google less predictable, more improvisational
- For Google solutions: one obvious + one innovative + third option
- High-level framework and signals are essentially the same

## Example Interview: Meta Volunteering Product

**Product Motivation (3.5/5):** "Connect to Contribute" concept. Mission: "To make volunteering opportunities as accessible as connecting with friends"

**Target Audience (4.5/5):**
- Ecosystem: volunteers, organizations, coordinators, beneficiaries
- Segments: Time-flexible regulars, Busy professionals (chosen), Students
- Persona: Sarah, 32, marketing manager

**Problem Identification (4/5):**
- Journey: Discovery → Evaluation → Scheduling → Volunteering → Post-volunteering
- Problems: Time-matching friction (chosen), Last-minute conflicts, Impact visibility gap

**Solution Development (4/5):**
- Solutions: Volunteer Time Bidding, Flash Volunteer, Micro-Volunteering Marketplace (chosen)
- MVP: Facebook Groups integration, time-chunking interface, sign-up system, basic handoff
- Risks: Organization resistance, quality/consistency

## Example Interview: Google Maps Parking

**Product Motivation:** Connected to Google's mission. Mission: "To help users navigate confidently and efficiently to any destination by providing comprehensive guidance from start to finish, including parking"

**Target Audience:**
- Ecosystem: Drivers, parking operators, cities, local businesses, property managers, tech providers
- Segments: Urban professionals in unfamiliar areas (chosen), Suburban errand-runners, Event seekers
- Persona: Sarah, 32, management consultant

**Problem Identification:**
- Journey: Pre-trip → En-route → Approaching → Finding parking → Walking → Returning → Exiting
- Problems: Uncertainty about availability (chosen), incomplete cost/restriction info, navigation ends at destination not parking

**Solution Development:**
- Solutions: Smart Parking Predictions (chosen), Crowdsourced Parking Finder, Parking Reservation Integration
- MVP: Focus on garages in 2-3 cities, simple availability indicators, historical patterns, user feedback

## Notes and Tips

- Layout the framework you will use first
- Product has no correct answers; show your process
- Set the North Star between Problem and Solution discovery
- **Be the driver of the interview — bring the interviewer along**
- **Use a prioritization framework like RICE**
- Be opinionated but open to debate
- Think out loud and explain your thought process
- **Interviews are a race against the clock to get the interviewer everything they need**
- **Read the interviewer and understand what they want**
- PM interviews tend to stay high level; EM interviews want more technical details
